Spotify Recommendation System Our project aimed to create a personalized song recommendation system using machine learning techniques. We started by applying PCA to reduce the dimensionality of our Spotify dataset, making it easier to work with. Then, we explored hierarchical clustering but ran into issues with overlapping clusters, which made it hard to interpret the results. To improve, we switched to K-Means clustering, which gave us clearer and more distinct clusters. Using features like loudness, instrumentality, and danceability, we built a system that takes an input song and suggests five similar tracks. Overall, the project helped us combine what we learned in class with real-world data to solve a problem we cared about. Dec. 2024
Supervised Learning Performance Analysis This research paper explores the performance of three machine learning classifiers—Decision Tree, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and Logistic Regression—on Autism, Fertility, and Liver datasets using different data partition splits (80/20, 50/50, and 20/80). The study aims to identify the best classifier and partition strategy for various datasets, highlighting strengths and weaknesses like SVM's ability to handle non-linear relationships and Decision Trees' tendency to overfit. Overall, the paper concludes that SVM paired with an 80/20 split delivers the most consistent results, but each model has its limitations depending on the dataset. The analysis emphasizes practical insights for real-world data applications, though there’s room for more experimentation to further validate the findings. Dec. 2024

Integrated OpenAI’s GPT-4o-mini to develop Dr. Qura, an AI-powered medical expert that provides concise drug summaries using natural language processing. Designed the app interface in Figma, focusing on accessibility for all age groups and ensuring a user-friendly experience. The project aimed to create a meaningful tool for users to access critical medication details, addressing health-related uncertainties with technology. Aug. 2024
Reducing Global Emissions In this project, we analyzed global emissions data from the Center for Energy Research at UCSD to address the critical issue of climate change. Using a dataset spanning 30 years, we focused on identifying the largest greenhouse gas-emitting regions—China, India, and the United States—and explored the potential of alternative energy sources like wind, solar, nuclear, and hydropower to reduce emissions. Our geographic visualization mapped emissions by region, highlighting the uneven distribution of emissions worldwide. Statistical analysis through ANOVA revealed significant regional and energy-type-based differences in emissions, emphasizing the need for tailored energy policies. Additionally, we considered ethical implications, such as potential biases in emissions data and the disproportionate impact of climate change on poorer countries. This work underscores the importance of leveraging data-driven insights to develop targeted strategies for reducing emissions and promoting sustainable energy solutions globally. June 2024
